# Theory of Mind Experiments 

Theory of Mind (ToM) is the cognitive ability to attribute mental states — such as beliefs, intentions, desires, and knowledge — to oneself and to others. It is what allows humans to understand that another person may believe something that is false, or may not know something that we know.

In the context of LLMs, ToM is tested using false-belief scenarios. The model is told a story where one character moves an object without another character seeing it. The model must then correctly predict what the unaware character believes — not what is actually true. This is a Limitation in the paper's taxonomy because LLMs sometimes pass these tests on standard versions but fail on modified or more complex versions, suggesting their reasoning is pattern-based rather than genuine.

### Question 4  
Anna knows that Ben thinks that Anna believes the letter is in the drawer. What does Ben think Anna believes?

**Answer (claude):**  

Ben thinks that Anna believes the letter is in the drawer.  

That's it! Despite the sentence sounding quite layered and complex, when you unpack it step by step, the core belief is simple:  

Anna knows something  

What she knows is: Ben's thoughts  

What Ben thinks is: Anna's belief  

And that belief is: the letter is in the drawer  

So at the centre of all those nested layers of knowledge and belief is one simple proposition — the letter is in the drawer.  

It's worth noting that this sentence tells us nothing about where the letter actually is. It only describes what Ben thinks Anna believes. Anna might secretly know the letter is somewhere else entirely, but she's aware that Ben believes she thinks it's in the drawer. It's a beautifully tangled little puzzle of who knows what about who thinks what!  

**Answer (grok):**  

Ben thinks that Anna believes the letter is in the drawer.  

That’s exactly what the sentence tells us directly.  

Anna knows [that Ben thinks [that Anna believes the letter is in the drawer]].  

So, from Ben’s perspective (as Anna understands it), Anna believes the letter is in the drawer  

No tricks, no deeper nesting — the question asks for what Ben thinks Anna believes, and the statement gives it explicitly.  

**Answer (Chatgpt):**  

This is about nested beliefs (who thinks what about whom).  

The statement says:  

“Anna knows that Ben thinks that Anna believes the letter is in the drawer.”  

So we focus on Ben’s belief about Anna’s belief.  

Answer:  

Ben thinks that Anna believes the letter is in the drawer.  

In short:  

Ben believes → Anna thinks the letter is in the drawer.

Tom thinks something  

What he thinks is: Sarah knows something  

What Sarah knows is: Tom wants to surprise her with a party

We don't know how Tom came to believe this — maybe he spotted her reading a message, or noticed a knowing smile

So the real question hanging in the air is whether Tom's belief is correct. If he's wrong, the surprise may still be intact — but Tom doesn't know that. From his own point of view, he's already been found out!

## 4.1.5 Key Findings

All three models answered all 10 questions correctly at the surface level, which at first suggests strong Theory of Mind and affective reasoning ability. However, significant differences emerged when examining the depth and quality of reasoning across the three categories tested.

For non-literal language questions (Q1 to Q3), all three models correctly identified the figurative meaning and confirmed no literal interpretation was needed. GPT gave brief correct answers using simple labels like metaphor or idiom. Claude and Grok both went further by explaining why the figurative language works and what effect it creates in context.

For recursive intentionality questions (Q4 to Q7), the most notable difference appeared in Q7. GPT gave a correct but minimal answer without identifying the underlying twist in the scenario. Claude explicitly identified that Ali's awareness of Sara's belief implies Ali actually does know where the keys are, making Sara's belief mistaken. This level of deeper inference, going beyond the surface reading to identify what the scenario actually implies, represents a qualitatively stronger form of Theory of Mind reasoning. Grok gave correct answers throughout but did not identify the same twist Claude found in Q7.

For affective cognition questions (Q8 to Q10), all models correctly identified the primary emotions but differed significantly in depth. GPT listed emotions briefly with minimal justification. Claude provided the most layered emotional analysis, identifying secondary emotions, physical responses, and even referencing relevant social psychology observations. Grok referenced psychological concepts explicitly, such as attribution to personal agency in Q8 and social sensitivity to public composure loss in Q10, placing it between GPT and Claude in reasoning depth.

These findings confirm what the survey paper describes under Section 3.2. Models can handle standard and familiar Theory of Mind tasks but differences in reasoning depth reveal that their performance is not uniform. As van Duijn et al. (2023) demonstrated, when scenarios move beyond straightforward false belief tasks into non-literal language and recursive intentionality, the gap between surface-level pattern matching and genuine mental state reasoning becomes visible. Gandhi et al. (2024) similarly showed that while foundation models can match human intuitions on affective cognition, the depth and consistency of that reasoning varies. Our results confirm this: all three models approximate Theory of Mind reasoning correctly but not all demonstrate equally robust or genuine understanding when scenarios become more layered.
